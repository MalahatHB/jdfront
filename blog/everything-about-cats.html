Everything About Cats

May 31, 2025  •  Published by Malahat

It is estimated that there are around 720 million pet or stray cats worldwide. These playful felines are believed to have been domesticated for the first time around 7500 BC, and today they have become beloved members of families across the globe. Their mysterious personalities and adorable appearance have made cats incredibly popular as pets. However, there is still so much we don’t know about these cute and playful animals. The History of the Relationship Between Cats and Humans

Researchers have recently discovered that cats lived among people in Mesopotamia, Turkey, and the island of Cyprus in the Mediterranean thousands of years before they appeared in Egypt. The ancient Egyptians began domesticating the African wildcat around 4,000 years ago. In ancient Egypt, people believed cats were magical, divine, and godlike. In fact, they worshipped a feline goddess named Bastet, who was depicted as half-cat, half-woman.

Around 3,000 BCE, people in China also began domesticating cats. During the Song Dynasty (960–1279), cats were cherished as loyal pets. Artists depicted them in paintings and sculptures, and Chinese culture viewed cats as symbols of good luck. However, during the Middle Ages in Europe, cats were often associated with evil, darkness, and witchcraft. As a result, popes and high-ranking authorities condemned them, and cats were frequently tortured and killed.

Cats arrived in North and South America alongside European settlers. As the settlers began farming, cats helped forge a unique human-animal bond by using their natural hunting skills to drive away rodents and by serving as loyal companions. Today, with nearly 74 million pet cats in homes, the domestic cat is the most popular pet worldwide.

Cat Domestication

This process shouldn't be considered traditional "domestication"! Cats have not undergone major changes during their domestication and have retained much of the appearance and behavior of their wild ancestors. These animals are still fully capable of surviving in the wild. There are two main theories regarding the domestication of cats. The first is that they were used to control pests, and the second relates to friendly interactions between humans and cats.

The second theory is more likely, at least in the early stages of domestication, because if the goal had been strictly pest control, other animals such as mongooses or dogs would have been more effective and efficient. In both scenarios, several characteristics of cats—such as their small size, social nature, body language, love of play, high intelligence, and perhaps an innate tendency among felines to become tame—may have facilitated their domestication.

Strictly speaking, most cats aren't truly domesticated. However, one of the main appeals of owning cats in the modern world is that, despite being tame, they haven't changed much from their wild relatives and have retained their fundamental traits. Domestic cats also tolerate intense heat and show no signs of discomfort until their skin temperature exceeds 52°C (125.6°F).

Introduction to Cat Breeds

The International Cat Association (TICA), the world’s largest genetic registry of pedigreed cats, recognizes 73 different breeds. However, the Cat Fanciers’ Association (CFA), the largest cat registry in the United States, recognized 45 breeds in 2022. The Fédération Internationale Féline (FIFe), with member organizations in 40 countries, recognizes 48 breeds divided into four categories.

There are several reasons why different organizations recognize different numbers of cat breeds. The simplest reason lies in their general classification methods. Some registries do not include non-pedigreed cats whose ancestry doesn’t go back a certain number of generations. In addition, some associations group several types of cats into one category, while others separate them into distinct breeds. As a result, there is no exact number for how many cat breeds exist.

A Closer Look at Stray Cats

Stray cats live short and difficult lives on the streets. These cats never die of old age. Contagious diseases such as calicivirus, feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), feline leukemia (FeLV), and feline infectious peritonitis (FIP) are common among cats left outdoors. Minor cuts or simple wounds can develop into severe infections and lead to various feline illnesses. Upper respiratory infections can cause a cat’s eyes and nose to become so affected that they struggle to see or breathe.

Others die due to blood loss or anemia caused by worms, fleas, ticks, or untreated injuries. Urinary tract infections—often leading to urinary blockages in male cats—can result in a slow and agonizing death if left untreated.

Even if a cat miraculously escapes these dangers, it may still fall victim to painful deaths at the hands of cruel individuals. On the other hand, a feral cat may rarely learn to accept human companionship and live in a home. This is because a home is not a natural environment for a wild cat, and such a setting can be far more stressful than life outside in a colony. However, neutering and spaying services for stray cats can be an effective way to manage their population in urban areas.

Are children safe around pets?

Here are some useful tips to ensure that your children and pets have a safe and happy relationship:

  • Never leave a toddler unattended. Accidents can happen even with the most trustworthy pets.
  • Never approach any animal while it is eating, sleeping, or even when it is caring for its babies.
  • Always pet an animal gently, without pulling, and never from behind.
  • Avoid loud noises or sudden movements when approaching an animal.
  • If an adult says the pet is friendly, extend your hand with the palm facing up and gently move it toward the pet’s nose to let it sniff you.
  • Do not touch the animal’s eyes, ears, nose, mouth, or genitals.
  • Avoid contact with dog or cat feces, which can transmit diseases. Young children are especially tempted to put their hands into the cat’s litter box, but this is dangerous.
  • Do not allow your child to touch or stand close to the pet when the child is overly excited.
  • Do not allow your child to ride on large pets such as dogs.
  • Make sure your children understand that a pet is a living creature that deserves respect. Animals have their own needs and feelings. Therefore, teach your children from an early age how to interact with pets. You can get necessary guidance from your veterinarian.

How to care for your pet in cold weather?

Did you know that cold weather can pose a serious threat to your pet? Fur alone does not provide complete warmth, which is why caring for cats and other pets in cold weather is very important. Be aware of how your pet tolerates cold conditions. Thick blankets and towels are a great way to help keep your pet warm after coming inside from the cold.

Short-haired pets feel the cold faster because they have less protection. Pets with short legs and bodies closer to the ground feel the cold more quickly since their bodies have more contact with snow or ice.

Cold weather can worsen some medical conditions such as arthritis. For example, pets with diabetes or heart disease may have trouble regulating their body temperature during winter. Also, after every walk, dry your pet’s paws and belly to remove ice, salt, and chemicals. Dehydration in cold weather can be life-threatening. If your pets are outside, make sure their water bowls are filled with clean water.

Water in puddles or lakes can cause digestive problems for your pet and may even carry parasites or toxins. Chemicals used for melting snow or protecting gardens in winter are dangerous for pets. We all know that warm car engines can be a hazard to pets, so make sure your cat is not sleeping under the car.

Laser Play with Cats: Yes or No?

Cats need enrichment and environmental stimulation to help them exercise and stay healthy. Playing with laser pointers can be a fun activity when used properly. However, shining the laser directly into your cat’s eyes can harm their vision. Laser light can burn a cat’s retina. According to the American Academy of Ophthalmology, the higher the wattage of the laser light, the more dangerous it is—even brief exposure can cause damage.

When playing laser games with your cat, follow these safety precautions:

  • Use a low-wattage laser pointer with a maximum output of 5 milliwatts.
  • Never shine the light directly into your cat’s eyes.
  • Store the laser toy in a safe place where your cat cannot access it.
  • These guidelines apply to all light sources, including flashlights, which your cat may also enjoy chasing the bright spots from.

Playing with laser toys may also affect your furry friend’s mental well-being. Toys like laser pointers can cause frustration. Since cats are natural hunters, they become frustrated if they cannot experience the actual catch when pouncing on their prey. The reason cats are attracted to laser pointers is that the quick movements of the light mimic those of a living creature. Therefore, it is recommended that after each “hunt” with the laser pointer, you give your cat a small piece of food or treat to simulate a successful catch and prevent frustration.

Cats perceive the moving dot as a living thing worth catching. If your cat chases the laser light without paying attention to their surroundings and bumps into walls or furniture, laser pointers become a potential hazard. If you decide to use a laser pointer, make sure to do so in an open space where your cat is unlikely to get hurt.

Do cats have night vision?

Cats cannot see in complete darkness, but their vision is far more advanced than humans’ for all their nighttime adventures. The reason cats have excellent night vision lies in the clever design of their eyes. These animals have curved corneas and large lenses, and in low light, their pupils can dilate into almost perfect circles to maximize the amount of light entering their eyes.

In addition, the photoreceptors in cats’ eyes are quite different from those in humans. Cats have more rod cells, which are responsible for peripheral vision and detecting movement at night, while humans have more cone cells that help them see colors and daylight better.

Cats also have a layer of cells called the tapetum lucidum, a reflective layer that bounces light back onto the retina. This tissue reflects light back to the sensory cells, allowing the retina to receive more than 50% of the available light. This layer is also the reason cats’ eyes shine in the dark.

You may have often wondered why your cat’s pupils appear as vertical slits during the day, but become fully round at night or when they feel playful. This happens because vertical slit pupils allow less light to enter the eye during bright conditions, protecting the retinal cells from damage, and dilate widely in low light to let in more light. Interestingly, cats have a field of vision of about 200 degrees, while humans have around 180 degrees. This means cats have a wider field of view.

Final Word

Having a cat can mean different things to different people. Some want a cat to cuddle or sit on their lap. Others enjoy playing with their cat. The important thing is to try to find a cat that connects well with you. Not all cats are the same, and how each cat behaves with you can depend on its inherent personality and early experiences. In any case, you should increase your knowledge about this animal so that you can both take good care of your cat and have a positive, constructive relationship with your furry friend.
